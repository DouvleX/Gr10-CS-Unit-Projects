Picking your parts:

Believe it or not, the hardest part about building a computer is picking your parts, not actually building the machine. The reason for this is you want to make sure the parts you have picked satisfy your needs because the last thing you want when building your first gaming computer is to have buyers remorse or worse have incompatible parts. Also you want to make sure your parts are compatible with each other because many parts are incompatible with other parts (so for example an AMD Ryzen CPU won't be compatble with an Intel LGA1151 motherboard). To help you with picking your parts you should use PC Part picker. This website detects if any of your parts are compatible or not, shows you the cheapest price from many reputable vendors, has many options for parts and a very simple layout that allows easy navigation around the site. Usually in a gaming computer, your graphics card is usually the most expensive part of your computer followed by your processor. This is because your graphics card does most of the processing required to render your game and make it look graphically immersive

CPU (Processor):

The processor is basically the brain of your computer,
it handles basically all of the tasks that go on in your
computer, and assigns tasks to different parts. However,
it doesn't impact your gaming experience as much as a
graphics card because the graphics card is what renders
things on your screen, and even though some CPUs have
integrated graphics processing units, they're not nearly
as strong as a good graphics card. For entry level gaming
2 cores is enough however it is highly recommended to have
a 4 core CPU as they also help on higher resolutions and
day to day tasks. You may also need a CPU cooler depending
on whether you're planning to overclock or not, however
most CPUs come with coolers in the box (except Intels K
series). Below is a chart to help in picking your CPU

Motherboard:

The motherboard is what houses all of your internal parts, and
sends information to other parts. While using PCpartpicker,
after picking your CPU it automatically filters out
incompatable motherboards so don't worry about that. The
motherboard really isn't that important as it doesn't impact
your gaming experiance or day to day usage too much. So just
pick one that is reliable, looks nice, and has many features
at a cheap price from reputable vendors such as MSI, Gigabyte,
and ASUS.

MSI Z270 Gaming motherboard
RAM (Random Access Memory):

The RAM is very fast storage that is used by the CPU to store
code while the CPU is doing other things. As long as you
get more than 8GB it won't effect your gaming experiance
too much. So like the motherboard, just buy the cheapest
most reliable, and fastest RAM sticks available at the time.

G.SKILL Ripjaws V Series 16GB (2 x 8GB)
Storage:

When it comes to storage you have 2 options, you can either
choose a hard drive and have a lot of storage but lose
out on speed, or pick an SSD and have a small amount of
GBs but have very fast speeds. Although there is other
technologies such as hybrid hard drive which is a normal
hard drive with a small very fast flash memory partition,
and NVMe drives which dont use traditional SATA but instead
are plugged into an NVMe slot, the 2 mentioned above are
the most common. However you can have the best of both
worlds by using a SSD as a boot drive to run your common
apps and windows, and then a hard drive for mass storage
for things such as pictures and other large apps.

GPU (Graphics processing unit):

The GPU is by far the most important part in your gaming
computer. You definatly do not want to cheap out in this
area as this is what is going to render all of your games,
and since you're building a "gaming" computer you want
to make sure your games look nice. The 2 most popular
GPU vendors are Nvidia and AMD. Nvidia usually has
stronger cards that are reliable and packed with more
features, however they come at a more expensive price.
On the other side however, AMD has basic features
however they offer very good performance for their price.

PSU (Power supply unit):

The power supply is what powers all of your parts
from case fans to your CPU. You definatly don't
want to cheap out in this area because the last
thing you want is your computer to blow up while
you're using it. To determine how reliable a PSU
a rating system was made called the 80 plus system.
It's highly recommended to get a power supply with
a rating of atleast 80+ bronze.

Corsair VS450 450Watt PSU
Case:

The case is what houses all of your parts. It doesnt
really help with your gaming experiance, however
you want to make sure your case fits your graphics
card, your CPU cooler, and has great airflow to make
sure your parts stay cool and dont overheat. Also you
want to make sure your case is easy to work in and
has good cable management options. Other than that
just pick whichever case you think looks nice
